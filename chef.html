Grant Achatz (of Alinea in Chicago) has a dish called “Pheasant, Shallot, Cider, Burning Oak Leaves”. It’s a single bite encased in tempura batter, eaten directly off a small oak branch, smoldering leaves attached. As the stories go: some diners, upon encountering the smell of burning leaves, become suddenly very quiet. They take the bite - finding on their tongue the remaining, more corporeal sensations of a brisk autumn in their childhood - and burst into tears. These stories are great exaggerations, I’m sure, but they highlight the ambitions of the cuisine at Alinea: to invoke feeling, and to communicate palpable ideas. Alinea’s is a style of cooking sometimes dismissed as gimmicky or sterile, but to me and the rest of their fans, avant-garde cuisine is more expressive and invigorating than any other.

My girlfriend, Nora, spent her first year of college in Italy. It was a nightmare. But, like any other, it ended; and with that in sight, I decided to put together a dinner in celebration of her return - a culmination of everything I had learned about cooking, and, if done right, a meditation on Nora’s experiences and identity. I had done something like this the year before, when she graduated high school, but my ambitions were set much higher for this dinner, and I was confident I had practiced and learned enough about cooking to match them.

The process of actually creating the menu and recipes was extremely difficult. On the one hand, it was a writing process: involving lots of 3x5 cards, sprawling conjecture and brainstorming in my notebook, and lots of pensive walks in nature. On the other hand, it was a logistics nightmare: it’s very difficult for one person to serve upwards of ten courses to six people, so I had to do as much ahead as I possibly could. I began cooking four days before the meal, and when it was finally over, I heaved a sigh of relief that the promise of a big dinner had kept Nora from running off with some Italian guy. Alternately: she’s a decent person who loves me, too. Either way, I’m immensely proud of what I managed to pull off.

An intimate event like this is so helpful in scratching that culinary creative itch. The possibilities presented when you know every one of your diners, when you have them as a captive audience for an hour or two, are absolutely endless. The wonder of Alinea and other restaurants like it is that they reach people in this intimate way, though they have never met them before. The ultimate goal in this medium is to evoke truly universal ideas and feelings, that’s the skill the great Chefs have honed in on. Deliciousness should be a foregone conclusion.
